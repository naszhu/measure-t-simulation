# Lesson 13: CPE Part 2 Review Writing Mastery

## Class Notes

### 1. Introduction to Review Writing

**Today's Focus:**
- Understanding what makes a review distinct from other genres
- Balancing description with critical evaluation
- Developing a sophisticated reviewer's voice
- Using C2-level evaluative language and structures
- Meeting CPE assessment criteria for reviews

**The Review Challenge:**
Reviews are NOT simply descriptions with a positive or negative opinion tacked on. They're critical analyses that help readers make informed decisions by offering reasoned evaluation based on specific criteria.

---

### 2. Understanding the Review Genre

**Core Purpose:**
A review evaluates something (an event, product, service, performance) to help readers decide whether it's worth their time, money, or attention.

**Key Characteristics of Reviews:**
- **Dual function:** Inform AND persuade
- **Evidence-based:** Claims supported by specific details
- **Criteria-driven:** Evaluates against relevant standards
- **Reader-oriented:** Considers audience needs/interests
- **Balanced:** Acknowledges both strengths and weaknesses

**The Review Logic Chain:**
**Description** → **Analysis** → **Evaluation** → **Recommendation**

Each stage builds on the previous one to create a coherent argument.

---

### 3. How Reviews Differ from Other Genres

| Genre | Primary Purpose | Key Difference from Review |
|-------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Article** | Inform/entertain | More objective; evaluation not central |
| **Report** | Present information systematically | Formal structure; minimal personal voice |
| **Essay** | Discuss/argue | Abstract ideas vs. specific experience |
| **Review** | Evaluate & recommend | Personal experience + critical judgment |

**The Critical Distinction:**
A review must have a clear **evaluative stance**. You're not just describing what happened—you're making a judgment about its quality, value, or worth, and explaining WHY you reached that judgment.

---

### 4. The Anatomy of a C2 Review

**Essential Components:**

#### 1. Engaging Opening
**Function:** Hook the reader and establish context
- Set the scene with vivid details
- Signal your overall stance (without revealing everything)
- Establish your credibility/perspective

#### 2. Descriptive Foundation
**Function:** Provide enough information for evaluation to make sense
- Key details about what you're reviewing
- Relevant background or context
- Specific examples that support your evaluation

#### 3. Critical Evaluation
**Function:** Make reasoned judgments based on criteria
- Analyze strengths and weaknesses
- Support claims with specific evidence
- Show sophistication through nuanced judgment

---

### 5. The Anatomy of a C2 Review (Continued)

#### 4. Comparative Context (Optional but Sophisticated)
**Function:** Show broader perspective and judgment
- Compare to similar events/products/experiences
- Reference industry standards or expectations
- Demonstrate knowledge of the field

#### 5. Clear Conclusion & Recommendation
**Function:** Provide actionable guidance
- Summarize your overall assessment
- Make specific recommendations
- Consider different audience needs

**Key Principle:**
The balance between description and evaluation should be approximately **40% description** and **60% evaluation**. Description provides the foundation; evaluation is the heart of the review.

---

### 6. Recognizing Different Review Contexts

Reviews adapt their focus and criteria to different contexts:

**Event Review (concerts, exhibitions, performances):**
- Quality of performance/execution
- Atmosphere and audience experience
- Value for money
- Comparison to similar events

**Product Review (books, films, gadgets):**
- Functionality or effectiveness
- Design and usability
- Value proposition
- Target audience suitability

**Service Review (restaurants, hotels, courses):**
- Quality of service delivery
- Professional standards
- Customer experience
- Recommendation for specific needs

---

### 7. The Jobs Fair Review Task: Understanding Requirements

**Task Analysis:**
*"You recently attended a jobs fair organised for students in their final year at university by a number of major international companies. You decide to write a review of the event for your student newspaper. You should briefly describe one or two jobs which attracted you. Your review should also evaluate how well different companies marketed themselves at the jobs fair in order to attract potential employees."*

**Key Requirements:**
1. **Context:** Jobs fair for final-year students
2. **Publication:** Student newspaper
3. **Content Elements:**
   - Describe 1-2 specific jobs (briefly)
   - Evaluate company marketing effectiveness (primary focus)
4. **Purpose:** Help readers assess the event's value

---

### 8. Understanding "Marketing Effectiveness"

For the jobs fair task, "marketing" refers to how companies presented themselves, not advertising campaigns.

**What to Evaluate:**
- **Presentation:** Visual appeal, professional materials, booth design
- **Engagement:** Staff interaction, demonstrations, activities
- **Information delivery:** Clarity, completeness, accessibility
- **Atmosphere:** Welcoming, professional, approachable
- **Memorability:** What made certain companies stand out

**Not the Focus:**
- Detailed job descriptions (include briefly, but don't dominate)
- Company reputation or financial performance
- Your personal career preferences (unless relevant to effectiveness)

---

### 9. Developing Your Evaluative Voice

**Strong evaluative voices:**
- Make clear judgments while showing sophistication
- Acknowledge nuance and context
- Sound confident but not dogmatic
- Balance criticism with constructive insights

**Example Opening - Strong Evaluative Voice:**
"Last week's graduate jobs fair promised final-year students direct access to major employers. While some companies seized this opportunity with impressive marketing strategies, others squandered the chance through uninspired presentations and passive engagement."

**Why this works:**
- Sets clear evaluative stance early
- Uses sophisticated vocabulary ("seized," "squandered")
- Promises balanced assessment
- Engages reader with specific preview

---

### 10. Positive Evaluation - C2 Vocabulary

**Basic Terms to Avoid:**
good, nice, great, awesome, cool

**Sophisticated Alternatives:**

**Describing Success:**
- exemplary, outstanding, commendable, noteworthy
- exceeded expectations, proved effective, stood out
- demonstrated excellence, distinguished itself

**Describing Impressions:**
- sophisticated, polished, compelling, engaging
- struck the right balance, resonated strongly
- captured attention effectively

**Describing Impact:**
- made a lasting impression, generated considerable interest
- proved particularly effective, achieved its objectives
- successfully conveyed, clearly articulated

---

### 11. Negative Evaluation - C2 Vocabulary

**Basic Terms to Avoid:**
bad, boring, terrible, not good

**Sophisticated Alternatives:**

**Describing Disappointment:**
- disappointing, lackluster, underwhelming
- fell short, left much to be desired, failed to impress

**Describing Problems:**
- proved problematic, raised concerns
- fell below expectations, lacked the necessary impact
- demonstrated significant weaknesses, suffered from notable flaws

**Describing Missing Elements:**
- overlooked, underestimated, missed opportunities
- failed to capitalize on, neglected to address
- lacked sufficient attention to

---

### 12. Nuanced Evaluation: The Mark of C2

C2 reviews show sophistication through qualification. Strong reviews rarely claim something is entirely perfect or completely terrible. Instead, they offer **nuanced judgment**.

**Qualification Techniques:**

#### 1. Acknowledging Strengths Within Weaknesses:
*"While the presentation material itself was impressively comprehensive, the delivery unfortunately lacked the dynamism needed to engage a young audience effectively."*

#### 2. Noting Limitations to Otherwise Positive Assessment:
*"The company's stall was undeniably professional, though perhaps overly formal for an event targeting recent graduates seeking creative industries."*

#### 3. Context-Dependent Evaluation:
*"The casual, relaxed approach might work well for startups targeting tech-savvy millennials, but seemed mismatched for a financial services company seeking to project stability and trustworthiness."*

---

### 13. Criteria-Based Evaluation

**Why Criteria Matter:**
Sophisticated reviews evaluate against **relevant standards** rather than just expressing personal preferences. This shows analytical thinking.

**For the Jobs Fair Task, Relevant Criteria Include:**

| Criterion | What to Evaluate | Example Language |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Professionalism** | Staff conduct, appearance, organization | "exemplified professionalism," "maintained impeccable standards" |
| **Information Quality** | Clarity, completeness, accuracy | "provided comprehensive details," "offered transparent information" |
| **Engagement** | Interactive elements, accessibility | "effectively engaged visitors," "created interactive experiences" |
| **Presentation** | Visual appeal, organization, creativity | "striking visual presentation," "demonstrated creativity" |
| **Value Proposition** | Career opportunities, benefits clarity | "articulated clear career pathways," "outlined compelling benefits" |

---

### 14. Evidence-Supported Evaluation

**The Golden Rule:**
Never make an evaluative claim without supporting it with specific evidence.

**Weak vs. Strong Evidence Support:**

**❌ Weak: Unsupported Claims**
"The company had a good presentation. It was very professional and interesting. Many students were attracted to their stall."
*Problem: Vague adjectives without concrete details*

**✅ Strong: Evidence-Based Claims**
"The company's presentation impressed through its attention to detail—uniformed staff answered questions knowledgeably, interactive screens allowed visitors to explore career paths independently, and half-hourly presentations showcased company culture effectively. The consistently crowded stall testified to this approach's success."
*Success: Specific details support evaluative claims*

**Evidence Types to Include:**
- **Specific details:** "half-hourly presentations," "interactive screens"
- **Observable behaviors:** "staff patiently answered," "visitors crowded around"
- **Concrete examples:** "key rings, notepads, calendars"
- **Quantifiable elements:** "after only 2 years," "the biggest stall"

---

### 15. Structuring Your Review Effectively

**Recommended Structure for Jobs Fair Review:**

#### Paragraph 1: Engaging Opening (50-60 words)
- Set the scene: when, where, what kind of event
- Signal overall impression (positive/mixed/negative)
- Preview what you'll evaluate

*Example:* "Last week's graduate jobs fair at the university sports center promised final-year students direct access to major employers. With over fifty companies competing for attention, the event offered a fascinating study in corporate marketing strategies—some impressively effective, others surprisingly lackluster."

#### Paragraph 2: First Company - Positive Example (70-80 words)
- Brief job description
- Detailed marketing evaluation with specific evidence
- Explain WHY approach was effective

#### Paragraph 3: Second Company - Another Perspective (70-80 words)
- Brief job description
- Different marketing approach evaluation
- May be positive or mixed evaluation

---

### 16. Structuring Your Review (Continued)

#### Paragraph 4: Comparative/Contrast Analysis (60-70 words)
- Discuss weaker examples (without naming if negative)
- Identify what separated strong from weak
- Show understanding of effective marketing principles

*Example:* "In contrast, several companies relied on passive approaches—unmanned stalls with generic brochures and no interactive elements. These missed the fundamental principle that recruitment at job fairs requires active engagement, not simply information distribution. The contrast was stark: lively, crowded stalls versus largely ignored tables."

#### Paragraph 5: Conclusion & Recommendation (40-50 words)
- Overall assessment of the fair
- Clear recommendation
- Forward-looking perspective

*Example:* "Overall, the fair proved invaluable for students navigating increasingly competitive graduate recruitment. The standout companies demonstrated that effective job fair marketing requires professionalism, interactivity, and genuine engagement—lessons that future participants would do well to learn."

---

### 17. Register and Tone for Reviews

**The Review Register Sweet Spot:**
Reviews typically occupy a **semi-formal register**—professional and credible but also engaging and accessible.

**Register Calibration for Student Newspaper:**

| Appropriate ✓ | Why | Inappropriate ✗ | Why |
|--------------|-----|----------------|-----|
| "The presentation proved impressive" | Professional but accessible | "The presentation was lit" / "totally awesome" | Too informal/slangy |
| "Several companies fell short" | Clear evaluative language | "Several companies were rubbish" | Overly casual/harsh |
| "It is recommended that..." | Clear recommendation | "One must necessarily conclude that..." | Too formal/stiff |
| "Worth attending for anyone seeking..." | Helpful guidance | "Students seeking employment should attend" | Too directive/preachy |

---

### 18. C2 Vocabulary Precision in Reviews

**Word Choice Sophistication:**
C2 reviews demonstrate lexical range through **precise vocabulary choices** that convey exact nuances of meaning.

**Vocabulary Upgrade Examples:**

| Context | Basic | C2-Level |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Describing presentations | nice, good, cool | polished, slick, sophisticated, compelling, engaging, dynamic |
| Describing staff | friendly, nice, helpful | approachable, knowledgeable, accommodating, professional, articulate |
| Describing materials | good, useful, nice | comprehensive, informative, well-designed, visually appealing, thoughtfully crafted |
| Describing atmosphere | busy, popular, crowded | buzzing with activity, consistently well-attended, generated considerable interest |
| Describing problems | bad, boring, not good | underwhelming, lackluster, uninspired, fell short, missed the mark |

---

### 19. Advanced Grammar Structures for Reviews

**C2-Level Grammatical Features:**

#### 1. Participle Clauses for Concision:
"**Recognizing the importance of first impressions**, the company invested heavily in professional presentation materials."

"The staff, **dressed in matching uniforms and exuding confidence**, made a lasting impression on visitors."

#### 2. Inversion for Emphasis:
"**Rarely have I witnessed** such a stark contrast between professional and amateur approaches."

"**Not only did** the company provide comprehensive information, **but they also** created memorable interactive experiences."

#### 3. Cleft Sentences for Focus:
"**What distinguished** the successful companies **was** their genuine engagement with student concerns."

"**It was the interactive elements that** proved most effective in capturing attention."

---

### 20. Advanced Grammar Structures (Continued)

#### 4. Subjunctive/Conditional for Hypothetical Assessment:
"**Had the company invested** in more engaging presentation methods, their stall would likely have attracted more visitors."

"**Were I to attend** a similar event in future, I would prioritize companies demonstrating this level of professionalism."

#### 5. Fronted Adverbials for Sophisticated Flow:
"**Particularly impressive** was the company's use of technology to enhance visitor experience."

"**Less successfully**, several companies relied solely on printed materials, missing opportunities for meaningful interaction."

#### 6. Hedging Language for Nuanced Claims:
"The approach **appeared to be** effective, though **it could be argued that** a more targeted strategy might have yielded even better results."

"This strategy **seemed particularly well-suited** to the university setting."

---

### 21. Common Review Writing Pitfalls to Avoid

**❌ Pitfall 1: Description Without Evaluation**
**Problem:** "There were 50 companies at the fair. IBM had a large stall with computers and videos. A-Hold gave out free backpacks."
**Why it fails:** This is reporting, not reviewing. No judgment about quality or effectiveness.

**❌ Pitfall 2: Evaluation Without Evidence**
**Problem:** "The fair was excellent. All companies were really professional and interesting. I would definitely recommend it to other students."
**Why it fails:** Generic praise without specific supporting details makes claims unconvincing.

**❌ Pitfall 3: Missing the "Marketing" Analysis**
**Problem:** Spending 80% of review describing job roles and responsibilities without analyzing HOW companies presented themselves.
**Why it fails:** Doesn't address the task requirement to evaluate marketing effectiveness.

---

### 22. Common Review Pitfalls (Continued)

**❌ Pitfall 4: Inappropriate Register**
**Problem:** "The fair was lit! IBM was totally sick with their setup. Some companies were trash though lol."
**Why it fails:** While student newspaper allows some informality, this is too casual and lacks professionalism.

**❌ Pitfall 5: Overly Simplistic Evaluation**
**Problem:** "IBM was good. A-Hold was also good. Some companies were bad. Overall the fair was good."
**Why it fails:** No nuance, sophistication, or specific criteria. Sounds like a middle school review.

**❌ Pitfall 6: Ignoring Audience**
**Problem:** Writing as if for a business journal rather than student newspaper (overly technical, excessively formal).
**Why it fails:** Register doesn't match the specified publication context.

**Success Formula:**
**Specific Evidence + Evaluative Language + Appropriate Register + Analytical Thinking = Strong C2 Review**

---

### 23. Quick Reference: Evaluative Language Bank

**Positive Evaluation Expressions:**
- **Strength verbs:** excelled at, succeeded in, demonstrated excellence, proved effective, stood out, distinguished itself
- **Positive adjectives:** exemplary, commendable, impressive, sophisticated, polished, compelling, noteworthy, outstanding
- **Positive nouns:** highlight, strength, asset, advantage, accomplishment

**Negative Evaluation Expressions:**
- **Weakness verbs:** fell short, failed to impress, lacked impact, missed the mark, underestimated, overlooked
- **Negative adjectives:** disappointing, underwhelming, lackluster, uninspired, bland, generic, inadequate
- **Negative nouns:** shortcoming, weakness, flaw, oversight, missed opportunity

**Balanced/Nuanced Evaluation:**
- **Qualifying language:** while..., although..., despite..., nevertheless, however, on balance, to some extent
- **Hedging:** appeared to, seemed to, could be argued that, arguably, to a certain degree, relatively

---

### 24. Quick Reference: Review-Specific Transitions

**Moving from Description to Evaluation:**
- What made this particularly effective was...
- The strength of this approach lay in...
- This proved successful because...
- Where this fell short, however, was in...
- The real issue here was...

**Introducing Comparative Analysis:**
- In contrast to this professional approach...
- By comparison, other companies...
- While some companies succeeded, others...
- This stood in stark contrast to...
- What distinguished the successful companies from the rest was...

**Signaling Overall Assessment:**
- Overall, the event proved...
- On balance, I would assess...
- Taking everything into consideration...
- The verdict? / The bottom line?
- All things considered, this...
