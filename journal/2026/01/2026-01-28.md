# Background

## 1. The two research traditions

### 1.1 The prospective-memory line

PM research studies a very specific problem:

A person has already formed an intention.

The intention must later be executed when the right event or context appears.

The central questions are therefore:

- how the delayed intention is maintained
    
- how the person notices the right retrieval opportunity
    
- how the intended action wins against the ongoing task
    
- what control cost is paid before the target appears
    

This literature developed through several stages.

#### Stage A. Classic PM work: monitoring versus spontaneous retrieval

The classic PM literature separated two broad possibilities.

- One possibility is that participants strategically monitor for PM cues.
    
- The other is that some cues trigger retrieval relatively spontaneously.
    

The focal versus nonfocal distinction became central here.

- **Focal PM cue**: the ongoing task already processes the feature that defines the PM target.
    
- **Nonfocal PM cue**: the PM target depends on a feature not naturally processed by the ongoing task.
    

This matters because focal cues often show smaller ongoing-task costs, whereas nonfocal cues more often require active monitoring.

#### Stage B. Cost debates

A large part of the PM field debated what PM costs mean.

When ongoing-task RT slows in PM blocks, does that mean:

- resources are being shared away from the ongoing task?
    
- thresholds are being raised to delay responding and give PM retrieval more time?
    
- or both?
    

This moved the field toward formal evidence-accumulation accounts.

#### Stage C. Delay theory and PMDC

A major shift happened when PM costs were modeled with evidence-accumulation frameworks.

The important move here was:

PM cost no longer had to mean vague “resource consumption”.

It could be decomposed into parameters such as:

- threshold changes
    
- accumulation-rate changes
    
- selective inhibition
    
- task-priority effects
    

PMDC then extended this by modeling both ongoing-task and PM decisions within one system.

This is important for your goal because it provides a language for distinguishing:

- **proactive control**
    
- **reactive control**
    
- **capacity limits**
    
- **task priority / importance**
    

#### Stage D. Newer PM directions relevant to you

Three newer directions matter most for your current project.

##### (i) PM in demanding multitask environments

Later PMDC work showed that threshold-only stories are not always enough.

In simple paradigms, PM costs can often look mostly like threshold adjustment.

In more demanding paradigms, capacity-sharing and changes in processing quality can emerge (e.g., Strickland et al., 2019; Boag et al., 2019), whereas simpler PM paradigms have often been explained primarily by threshold-based proactive control rather than capacity sharing (e.g., Strickland et al., 2018; Boag et al., 2019).

This is directly relevant because the multiple-cue paradigm is already a competition-heavy task, so adding PM may push the system into the regime where capacity limits actually show up.

##### (ii) Context-sensitive PM control

Recent PMDC extensions show that PM control depends on context relevance.

This means PM is not just about “remembering a delayed intention”.

It is also about dynamically changing thresholds and evidence flow depending on whether the current context makes the PM task relevant.

That makes PM highly relevant to any theory that already talks about context match and importance weighting.

##### (iii) Individual differences and WM / controlled attention

The PM literature also suggests that focal and nonfocal PM are not identical constructs.

Nonfocal PM is more tied to controlled attention and monitoring.

Focal PM is more compatible with retrieval that piggybacks on ongoing-task processing.

So working memory is relevant, but not as a single global explanation of PM.

It is one contributor among several.

### 1.2 The MIS / multiple-cue / genBC line

Your group’s line starts from a different question.

The core issue is not primarily delayed intention retrieval.

The core issue is:

How does one among several competing action representations become selected for execution?

#### Stage A. The long-term-memory-for-intentions framing

The earlier intention model is already close to PM in one specific way.

It asks whether intentions can be stored in long-term memory and later retrieved, rather than merely maintained in working memory.

So there is already a conceptual bridge to PM.

But the emphasis is still different.

The emphasis is on the architecture of intention representations and their selection.

#### Stage B. The multiple-cue paradigm

The multiple-cue paradigm makes the competition problem concrete.

Several candidate actions are simultaneously available.

Their importance differs.

Reward and number of competitors affect which action is selected.

That gives you a clean laboratory tool for studying biased competition between actions.

#### Stage C. The generalized biased-competition framework

The newer genBC framing broadens the theory.

Instead of being about one specialized task, it becomes a more general architecture:

action representations compete based on:

- match to current context
    
- importance / weight
    

This is where the bridge to PM becomes genuinely promising.

Because PM also concerns an action representation whose relevance depends on future context and current priority.

## 2. The exact relation between PM and MIS

## They are related, but not identical.

That is the cleanest answer.

### 2.1 What PM studies

PM usually assumes:

- an intention has already been encoded
    
- there is an ongoing task occupying behavior
    
- some later event or context must trigger the intended action
    

So PM is mainly about:

- delayed retrieval
    
- monitoring
    
- cue detection
    
- switching / interruption
    
- coordination between ongoing-task action and delayed intention
    

### 2.2 What MIS studies

MIS is mainly about:

- competition among candidate action representations
    
- how context and importance bias that competition
    
- which action becomes active in working memory / execution
    

### 2.3 Where they overlap

They overlap at the level of **action selection under competition**.

A PM intention can be viewed as one special kind of action representation that must compete with ongoing-task actions when the relevant cue or context appears.

So PM can be treated as one **application domain** of a more general action-selection theory.

### 2.4 Where they differ

They differ in temporal structure and theoretical target.

PM cares about:

- how a previously formed intention survives a delay
    
- what kind of cue processing retrieves it
    
- why ongoing-task cost occurs
    

MIS cares about:

- how multiple candidate action representations are selected now
    
- how reward / importance / context bias the competition
    

So PM is not a direct synonym for “intention formation”.

Rather:

PM starts **after** intention formation and asks how the delayed intention later wins.

MIS can potentially cover the **selection stage** of PM, but PM contains extra structure that MIS does not automatically include.

## 3. Why the proposed experiment is interesting

The proposed experiment is strongest if it is treated as a **bridge experiment**.

Its value is that it can test whether PM demands alter multiple-cue selection through:

- threshold control
    
- rate / capacity control
    
- both
    
- or a more hierarchical interrupt-like mechanism
    

That is theoretically useful even if PM and MIS are not identical.

The experiment can answer questions such as:

- Does a delayed intention globally increase caution in MCP?
    
- Does nonfocal monitoring selectively reduce the quality of MCP evidence?
    
- Does PM priority behave like MCP reward, or does it behave differently?
    
- Is a PM response best modeled as another competitor in the same race, or as a higher-level interrupt / gate?
    

These are good questions.

So the project is not conceptually misguided.

But the current draft still mixes together three levels that should be separated.

- **Level 1:** PM as an empirical literature on delayed intentions.
    
- **Level 2:** MIS/genBC as a general action-selection architecture.
    
- **Level 3:** your specific experiment as a diagnostic tool for mechanisms.
    

The paper should explicitly separate these levels.
