# PM × Multiple-Cue Intention Selection: history, plausibility, and design advice

## Bottom line

The proposed experiment is **plausible and potentially valuable**, but only if it is framed carefully.

The strongest framing is **not** that prospective memory (PM) and MIS are the same thing.

The strongest framing is that PM provides a **well-developed process literature on delayed-intention control**, whereas MIS/genBC provides a **general architecture for competition among action representations**.

Your experiment becomes theoretically interesting when it asks whether a delayed intention introduced by a PM task changes multiple-cue value-based selection through the **same control currency** as MCP reward, or through a **different mechanism**.

So the real question is not:

“Is PM the same as intention formation?”

The real question is:

“Can PM manipulations reveal which control mechanisms drive selection in the multiple-cue paradigm, and where MIS should be expanded beyond its current scope?”

---

## 1. The two research traditions

### 1.1 The prospective-memory line

PM research studies a very specific problem:

A person has already formed an intention.

The intention must later be executed when the right event or context appears.

The central questions are therefore:

- how the delayed intention is maintained
    
- how the person notices the right retrieval opportunity
    
- how the intended action wins against the ongoing task
    
- what control cost is paid before the target appears
    

This literature developed through several stages.

#### Stage A. Classic PM work: monitoring versus spontaneous retrieval

The classic PM literature separated two broad possibilities.

- One possibility is that participants strategically monitor for PM cues.
    
- The other is that some cues trigger retrieval relatively spontaneously.
    

The focal versus nonfocal distinction became central here.

- **Focal PM cue**: the ongoing task already processes the feature that defines the PM target.
    
- **Nonfocal PM cue**: the PM target depends on a feature not naturally processed by the ongoing task.
    

This matters because focal cues often show smaller ongoing-task costs, whereas nonfocal cues more often require active monitoring.

#### Stage B. Cost debates

A large part of the PM field debated what PM costs mean.

When ongoing-task RT slows in PM blocks, does that mean:

- resources are being shared away from the ongoing task?
    
- thresholds are being raised to delay responding and give PM retrieval more time?
    
- or both?
    

This moved the field toward formal evidence-accumulation accounts.

#### Stage C. Delay theory and PMDC

A major shift happened when PM costs were modeled with evidence-accumulation frameworks.

The important move here was:

PM cost no longer had to mean vague “resource consumption”.

It could be decomposed into parameters such as:

- threshold changes
    
- accumulation-rate changes
    
- selective inhibition
    
- task-priority effects
    

PMDC then extended this by modeling both ongoing-task and PM decisions within one system.

This is important for your goal because it provides a language for distinguishing:

- **proactive control**
    
- **reactive control**
    
- **capacity limits**
    
- **task priority / importance**
    

#### Stage D. Newer PM directions relevant to you

Three newer directions matter most for your current project.

##### (i) PM in demanding multitask environments

Later PMDC work showed that threshold-only stories are not always enough.

In simple paradigms, PM costs can often look mostly like threshold adjustment.

In more demanding paradigms, capacity-sharing and changes in processing quality can emerge (e.g., Strickland et al., 2019; Boag et al., 2019), whereas simpler PM paradigms have often been explained primarily by threshold-based proactive control rather than capacity sharing (e.g., Strickland et al., 2018; Boag et al., 2019).

This is directly relevant because the multiple-cue paradigm is already a competition-heavy task, so adding PM may push the system into the regime where capacity limits actually show up.

##### (ii) Context-sensitive PM control

Recent PMDC extensions show that PM control depends on context relevance.

This means PM is not just about “remembering a delayed intention”.

It is also about dynamically changing thresholds and evidence flow depending on whether the current context makes the PM task relevant.

That makes PM highly relevant to any theory that already talks about context match and importance weighting.

##### (iii) Individual differences and WM / controlled attention

The PM literature also suggests that focal and nonfocal PM are not identical constructs.

Nonfocal PM is more tied to controlled attention and monitoring.

Focal PM is more compatible with retrieval that piggybacks on ongoing-task processing.

So working memory is relevant, but not as a single global explanation of PM.

It is one contributor among several.

---

### 1.2 The MIS / multiple-cue / genBC line

Your group’s line starts from a different question.

The core issue is not primarily delayed intention retrieval.

The core issue is:

How does one among several competing action representations become selected for execution?

#### Stage A. The long-term-memory-for-intentions framing

The earlier intention model is already close to PM in one specific way.

It asks whether intentions can be stored in long-term memory and later retrieved, rather than merely maintained in working memory.

So there is already a conceptual bridge to PM.

But the emphasis is still different.

The emphasis is on the architecture of intention representations and their selection.

#### Stage B. The multiple-cue paradigm

The multiple-cue paradigm makes the competition problem concrete.

Several candidate actions are simultaneously available.

Their importance differs.

Reward and number of competitors affect which action is selected.

That gives you a clean laboratory tool for studying biased competition between actions.

#### Stage C. The generalized biased-competition framework

The newer genBC framing broadens the theory.

Instead of being about one specialized task, it becomes a more general architecture:

action representations compete based on:

- match to current context
    
- importance / weight
    

This is where the bridge to PM becomes genuinely promising.

Because PM also concerns an action representation whose relevance depends on future context and current priority.

## 2. The exact relation between PM and MIS

## They are related, but not identical.

That is the cleanest answer.

### 2.1 What PM studies

PM usually assumes:

- an intention has already been encoded
    
- there is an ongoing task occupying behavior
    
- some later event or context must trigger the intended action
    

So PM is mainly about:

- delayed retrieval
    
- monitoring
    
- cue detection
    
- switching / interruption
    
- coordination between ongoing-task action and delayed intention
    

### 2.2 What MIS studies

MIS is mainly about:

- competition among candidate action representations
    
- how context and importance bias that competition
    
- which action becomes active in working memory / execution
    

### 2.3 Where they overlap

They overlap at the level of **action selection under competition**.

A PM intention can be viewed as one special kind of action representation that must compete with ongoing-task actions when the relevant cue or context appears.

So PM can be treated as one **application domain** of a more general action-selection theory.

### 2.4 Where they differ

They differ in temporal structure and theoretical target.

PM cares about:

- how a previously formed intention survives a delay
    
- what kind of cue processing retrieves it
    
- why ongoing-task cost occurs
    

MIS cares about:

- how multiple candidate action representations are selected now
    
- how reward / importance / context bias the competition
    

So PM is not a direct synonym for “intention formation”.

Rather:

PM starts **after** intention formation and asks how the delayed intention later wins.

MIS can potentially cover the **selection stage** of PM, but PM contains extra structure that MIS does not automatically include.

## 3. Why the proposed experiment is interesting

The proposed experiment is strongest if it is treated as a **bridge experiment**.

Its value is that it can test whether PM demands alter multiple-cue selection through:

- threshold control
    
- rate / capacity control
    
- both
    
- or a more hierarchical interrupt-like mechanism
    

That is theoretically useful even if PM and MIS are not identical.

The experiment can answer questions such as:

- Does a delayed intention globally increase caution in MCP?
    
- Does nonfocal monitoring selectively reduce the quality of MCP evidence?
    
- Does PM priority behave like MCP reward, or does it behave differently?
    
- Is a PM response best modeled as another competitor in the same race, or as a higher-level interrupt / gate?
    

These are good questions.

So the project is not conceptually misguided.

But the current draft still mixes together three levels that should be separated.

- **Level 1:** PM as an empirical literature on delayed intentions.
    
- **Level 2:** MIS/genBC as a general action-selection architecture.
    
- **Level 3:** your specific experiment as a diagnostic tool for mechanisms.
    

The paper should explicitly separate these levels.

## 4. Evaluation of the current proposed writing

## Overall assessment

The draft has a real theoretical core.

It is not superficial.

It is trying to distinguish mechanisms rather than just add another dual-task manipulation.

That is a major strength.

But it currently overstates some links, under-justifies some design decisions, and contains several confounds that would weaken the argument if not repaired.

### 4.1 Main strengths

#### Strength 1. It asks a mechanistic question, not just a behavioral one.

The best part of the draft is that it does not stop at “PM slows MCP performance”.

It asks whether the slowing reflects:

- thresholds
    
- capacity sharing
    
- dual mechanisms
    

That is exactly the right level of ambition.

#### Strength 2. Focal versus nonfocal is a meaningful manipulation.

This is a strong choice because PM literature has built a large theory around focality.

So it gives you a principled way to manipulate the likelihood of monitoring.

#### Strength 3. The design tries to force contact between two literatures.

This is good.

The project is not merely importing PM vocabulary.

It is using PM to stress-test the mechanism story in MCP/MIS.

That is publishable in principle.

---

### 4.2 Main weaknesses

#### Weakness 1. Baseline is confounded with time and training.

This is the single biggest design problem.

In the current draft, baseline MCP is collected earlier, and both PM conditions come later.

So PM cost is confounded with:

- additional practice
    
- greater automatization of color-key mappings
    
- fatigue across weeks
    
- attrition / motivation changes
    

Even if focal and nonfocal order is counterbalanced, baseline is still always earlier.

That means a difference between PM and baseline cannot be cleanly interpreted as PM cost.

#### Weakness 2. The focal manipulation is not perfectly clean.

Your “focal” PM cue is an orange border around a circle.

But in the MCP ongoing task, participants mainly need:

- circle identity by color
    
- numerical value inside the circle
    
- comparison among rewards
    

A border color is **related** to the visual object, but it is not literally the same feature required for the ongoing decision.

So reviewers may question whether this is truly focal in the strong PM sense.

It may be better described as **more overlapping** rather than fully focal.

#### Weakness 3. PM cues only appear on zero-value circles.

This creates a strong strategic confound.

Participants can learn that PM-relevant signals are restricted to the subset of circles that are already task-irrelevant for reward selection.

That changes the nature of monitoring.

Instead of monitoring the whole display, participants may only check low-value / zero-value circles.

That makes PM cost smaller and also ties PM detection to MCP display structure in a way that is hard to interpret.

#### Weakness 4. The current model may be too flat.

The proposed five-accumulator architecture treats PM as if it were just one more ordinary response option competing with red, blue, green, and yellow.

That may be too simple.

Why?

Because the PM response is functionally different from the ordinary MCP responses.

It behaves more like:

- a retrieval-triggered override
    
- or an interrupt signal
    
- or a gating mechanism that can cancel the normal choice
    

A flat five-way race is still worth trying.

But it should not be assumed to be the theoretically natural model.

A nested or hierarchical architecture may fit the logic better.

#### Weakness 5. The design is very long.

Thirteen sessions and about 2,020 total trials is heavy.

For expert longitudinal modeling this can be fine.

For a standard PM experiment with undergraduates, it risks:

- attrition
    
- unstable motivation
    
- strategic changes across weeks
    
- changes in how seriously the PM instruction is treated
    

The long design may be solving a modeling problem by creating a practical data-quality problem.

#### Weakness 6. Sample-size justification is currently too weak.

“30 (?)” is not enough as written.

With hierarchical modeling, many trials help.

But with attrition, exclusions, and model comparison across multiple mechanisms, the actual inferential question is not trivial.

You need simulation-based design work.

Specifically:

- parameter recovery
    
- model recovery
    
- estimated effect detectability under realistic noise
    

#### Weakness 7. The individual-differences add-on is not yet theoretically tight.

WM, processing speed, and inhibition are plausible.

But right now they look appended rather than necessary.

You need to say exactly why each measure maps onto a specific process in the candidate models.

Otherwise it looks like opportunistic measurement.

#### Weakness 8. The current story risks overclaiming that PM tells you what MIS parameters “mean”.

PM can constrain candidate mechanisms.

It cannot magically assign a unique semantic meaning to every MIS parameter.

Parameter interpretation must still come from:

- the formal model itself
    
- the mapping between task manipulations and parameters
    
- recovery / identifiability analyses

## 5. Direct answers to your concerns

### Concern 1.

You are unsure how PM and intention formation differ, what their research questions are, whether there is conflict, and what theory story to tell.

## Answer

There is **no necessary conflict**, but there is also **no exact identity**.

The correct relation is:

- PM is a literature about **remembering and executing delayed intentions**.
    
- MIS/genBC is a framework about **competition and selection among action representations**.
    

So PM is not the same as intention formation.

PM usually presupposes that an intention already exists.

MIS addresses which candidate action representation becomes selected.

The bridge is this:

A delayed intention in PM can be treated as one special action representation whose momentary competitive status changes when the cue/context appears.

That gives you a clean theory story:

> MIS/genBC is the more general competition architecture.  
> PM supplies a well-developed experimental tradition for delayed-intention retrieval and control.  
> The current experiment asks whether PM manipulations expose the control variables that also govern multiple-cue intention selection.

That is a coherent story.

Do **not** tell the story as “PM = intention formation”.

Tell it as “PM gives one structured case of future-oriented action control that can diagnose the control architecture assumed by MIS.”

---

### Concern 2.

Do you truly need PM literature to understand why parameters vary in MIS/genBC? Or is this just your memory-modeling habit?

## Answer

PM literature is **useful but not necessary in the strong sense**.

More exactly:

- You do **not** need PM literature to justify modeling per se in MIS/genBC.
    
- You **do** need some principled empirical literature if you want parameter changes to have clear psychological interpretation.
    

PM literature can help because it provides tested manipulations that map onto process hypotheses, especially:

- monitoring demand
    
- cue focality
    
- task priority
    
- delayed retrieval
    
- ongoing-task cost
    

So PM can help you with **mechanistic leverage**.

But it cannot fully solve parameter meaning by itself.

Why not?

Because MIS parameters belong to a different formal object:

competition among action representations in MCP.

A PM manipulation can tell you whether a parameter is sensitive to:

- monitoring burden
    
- strategic delay
    
- capacity limit
    
- priority
    

But it does not prove that the parameter “is” one of those things in all contexts.

So the right answer is:

PM literature can facilitate your modeling, but only as a **constraint source**, not as a one-to-one parameter dictionary.

---

### Concern 3.

Will adding PM actually help in the way you want?

## Answer

Yes, but only if the experiment is aimed at the right target.

If your goal is:

“Use PM because PM is another literature on intentions.”

then the benefit will be weak.

If your goal is:

“Use PM because PM has principled manipulations that selectively stress proactive control, reactive control, monitoring, capacity-sharing, and priority”

then the benefit is real.

So PM should be used as a **diagnostic perturbation** of MCP/MIS.

That is the true step.

Not because PM is conceptually identical.

But because PM lets you perturb the system in ways that are theoretically meaningful.

## 6. What the best current experiment should look like

## My recommendation: do a cleaner first bridge experiment, not the full maximal design.

The best next study is a **clean mechanism experiment**.

Not the longest possible design.

Not the most comprehensive one.

A cleaner design will tell you more.

### 6.1 The best first experiment

Use a within-subject block design with three block types:

- MCP-only baseline
    
- MCP + focal-ish PM
    
- MCP + nonfocal PM
    

And do this **within the same sessions**, with order counterbalanced.

That solves the major baseline confound.

#### Core principles

##### Principle 1. Baseline must be interleaved, not only earlier in the study.

You need clean no-PM blocks mixed with PM blocks within session or across matched sessions.

##### Principle 2. Focality should be made cleaner.

Try to make the focal cue rely on the exact feature already processed for the MCP decision.

If that is impossible, describe it as “high-overlap” rather than fully focal.

##### Principle 3. Do not restrict PM cues only to zero-value circles.

Better options:

- PM cues can appear equally across value positions, while PM response always overrides reward choice.
    
- Or use a separate contextual cue preceding the display, so PM relevance is orthogonal to the reward layout.
    

This matters a lot.

##### Principle 4. Keep the first study short enough that strategy remains stable.

A smaller number of sessions with enough trials per condition is preferable to a very long multiweek design.

##### Principle 5. Run parameter recovery before data collection.

This is mandatory for a paper centered on mechanistic claims.

---

### 6.2 What to manipulate first

For the first study, I would prioritize:

- PM presence / absence
    
- PM focality (or overlap)
    
- MCP cue competition level
    
- MCP reward difference
    

This is enough for a first bridge paper.

I would **not** yet add too many auxiliary measures.

---

### 6.3 What to model first

Fit at least three competing architectures.

#### Model A. Global-threshold modulation

PM increases response caution in MCP, but does not change evidence quality.

#### Model B. Capacity / evidence-quality modulation

Nonfocal PM reduces the quality or rate of evidence driving MCP selection.

#### Model C. Hierarchical interrupt model

PM is not just another flat response option.

Instead, PM cue detection triggers an interrupt or override process that competes at a higher level with normal MCP choice.

This third model is important.

Even if it loses, it is the right conceptual benchmark.

---

### 6.4 The best second experiment

Only after the first mechanism study works, run a second experiment that directly tests the bridge between PM priority and MCP reward.

For example:

- manipulate PM task importance
    
- manipulate MCP reward magnitude
    
- ask whether both influence the same model parameter or different ones
    

This would be the strongest direct test of whether PM priority and MCP value are instances of one common “importance” mechanism.

That is where the real contact with genBC becomes sharp.

---

## 7. Recommended theoretical claim for the eventual paper

A good claim would be:

> We do not treat prospective memory as identical to intention formation.  
> Instead, we use prospective-memory manipulations as mechanistically diagnostic perturbations of a multiple-cue intention-selection task.  
> This allows us to test whether delayed-intention demands alter value-based action selection through global threshold control, capacity-related changes in evidence quality, or a hierarchical interrupt mechanism.

This claim is strong, honest, and publishable.

A weaker claim would be:

> PM and MIS study the same thing.

That claim is too crude and will invite objections.

---

## 8. Final practical recommendation

If the goal is to get the best next experiment, I would do this:

### Version I would actually run

- 1 session of mapping/practice
    
- 2 or 3 experimental sessions
    
- each experimental session contains all core block types
    
- baseline blocks interleaved with PM blocks
    
- focal/high-overlap and nonfocal conditions counterbalanced within subject
    
- PM cues not restricted to zero-value circles
    
- no individual-difference tasks in the first paper unless they are central
    
- pre-run simulation for parameter recovery and model recovery
    
- compare flat-race versus hierarchical-interrupt accounts
    

### Main hypothesis

Nonfocal PM should impair MCP more than focal/high-overlap PM.

The critical question is whether that impairment looks like:

- mainly threshold change
    
- mainly evidence-quality change
    
- or a qualitatively different interrupt process
    

That result would immediately tell you something important about how MIS should be interpreted or extended.

---

## 9. Final answer in one sentence

The PM literature is worth bringing in, but not because PM and MIS are the same; it is worth bringing in because PM offers a mature set of mechanistic manipulations that can reveal whether multiple-cue intention selection is governed by common control variables, capacity-limited evidence dynamics, or a higher-level interrupt architecture.
