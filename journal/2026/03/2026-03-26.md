# Method

## Participants

We will recruit 30 adults from the University of Copenhagen participant pool. All participants will provide informed consent and receive a base compensation of xx DKK per session plus a performance-based bonus (0.01 DKK per point earned), yielding an expected total of approximately xx DKK across all sessions. We will exclude participants who show PM hit rates below 20% in either PM condition (indicating failure to engage with the PM task), MCP accuracy below 60% in the baseline phase (indicating misunderstanding of the task), or more than 15% anticipatory responses (RT < 200 ms).

## Apparatus and Stimuli

The experiment will be programmed in PsychoPy (v2023.1.3) and presented on a 24-inch LCD monitor at a 120 Hz refresh rate and 1920 × 1080 resolution. Participants will be seated approximately 60 cm from the screen in a dimly lit testing room and will respond using a standard keyboard.

### Display Layout

On each trial, three colored circles (150 px diameter, approximately 3.6° visual angle) appear at the vertices of an equilateral triangle centered on screen, with a center-to-circle distance of approximately 200 px (4.8°). The three spatial positions (top-center, bottom-left, bottom-right) are held constant throughout the experiment; assignment of colors to positions is randomized on every trial. Rewarded circles display a centrally placed black digit (1, 2, or 3) rendered in a bold sans-serif font (xx pt) indicating the reward value associated with responding to that color on the current trial. 

### Colors and Response Mapping

We use three colors selected from a colorblind-safe palette: red (RGB: 220, 50, 50), blue (RGB: 50, 100, 200), and green (RGB: 50, 170, 80). Each color is mapped to a fixed response key: red → D (left middle finger), blue → F (left index finger), green → J (right index finger). The left hand rests with the middle finger on D and the index finger on F; the right hand rests with the index finger on J. This three-key arrangement keeps all response fingers on the home row. The PM response is mapped to the spacebar, pressed with either thumb.

### MCP Trial Types

The experiment includes six colored cue paradigm trial types. Three are single-cue trials, in which one circle carries a reward value of 1, 2, or 3 and the other two display 0. Three are dual-cue trials, in which two circles carry different reward values while the third displays 0; the dual-cue value combinations are 2 vs. 1, 3 vs. 1, and 3 vs. 2. On every trial, the participant's task is to respond as quickly as possible with the key corresponding to the highest-reward color. On single-cue trials, only one response is rewarded; on dual-cue trials, the optimal response is always the higher-value color, but responding with the lower-value color is also counted (with its associated, smaller reward) rather than scored as an outright error. Responding with a zero-valued color is scored as incorrect.

### PM Cue Stimuli

Two types of PM cues are used, following the standard focal versus nonfocal distinction in the PM literature. The **focal PM cue** is an orange border (6 px thick, RGB: 255, 165, 0) surrounding one circle. Because participants are already processing the color of each circle for the MCP task, the addition of a salient color feature on the same dimension satisfies the criterion of focal processing: the ongoing task directs attention to the stimulus attribute that carries the PM cue. The **nonfocal PM cue** is a small white five-pointed star (★, approximately 20 px) rendered inside one circle, offset slightly above the reward digit. Detecting this cue requires monitoring a shape feature not otherwise processed for the MCP task, meeting the definition of nonfocal processing.

PM cues are distributed across all three circle positions with equal probability, regardless of whether a circle carries a nonzero reward value. When a PM cue appears on a rewarded circle, the participant faces a direct competition between the PM intention (press spacebar for 5 points) and the MCP intention (press the color key for the circle's reward value of 1, 2, or 3 points). Because the PM reward exceeds the maximum MCP reward, the PM response is always the value-maximizing choice on PM-present trials.

## Procedure

Participants complete 16 sessions distributed across four testing days, with sessions grouped as follows: Day 1 comprises five training sessions (approximately 3 hours); Day 2 comprises three baseline sessions and one PM session (approximately 3.5 hours); Day 3 comprises four PM sessions (approximately 3.5 hours); Day 4 comprises three PM sessions and an optional individual-differences battery (approximately 3–3.5 hours). Days are separated by 1–3 intervening days (no more than one week apart) to allow consolidation of color–key mappings while minimizing attrition.

### Training Phase (Sessions 1–5)

The training protocol follows a staged progression to ensure that color–key mappings are thoroughly automatized before the introduction of additional task demands.

**Session 1** (120 trials, four 30-trial blocks) presents a single colored circle at screen center on each trial, with a reward value of 1, 2, or 3. Only single-cue trials appear. A reference chart showing the color–key mapping is displayed at the bottom of the screen throughout this session to support initial learning. Feedback follows every trial: correct responses display "+[X] points!" in green; incorrect responses display "Wrong color −2 points" in red; responses exceeding 3000 ms display "TOO SLOW" in red.

**Session 2** (120 trials, four 30-trial blocks) moves the display to the three-circle triangular layout matching the experimental configuration, but maintains the single-cue structure (only one circle is rewarded per trial). The mapping chart remains visible.

**Session 3** (120 trials, four 30-trial blocks) introduces dual-cue trials for the first time. All six trial types (three single-cue, three dual-cue) appear with equal frequency (20 trials per type). The mapping chart remains visible as participants learn to handle competition between two rewarded options.

**Session 4** (120 trials, four 30-trial blocks) continues the full six-trial-type protocol but removes the on-screen mapping chart, requiring participants to rely entirely on memory for color–key associations.

**Session 5** (240 trials, four 60-trial blocks) provides extended practice with the full experimental protocol. This session uses the same trial distribution and timing as the upcoming baseline sessions. Performance must reach ≥ 75% accuracy on optimal selections (choosing the highest-value color) by the final block for the participant to proceed to baseline; participants not meeting this criterion complete an additional practice block.

### Baseline Phase (Sessions 6–8)

These three sessions establish baseline MCP performance without any PM demands. Each session consists of 480 trials organized into six 80-trial blocks, with mandatory 30-second rest breaks between blocks. The six trial types appear with equal frequency (80 trials per type per session). Feedback is identical to training. Across the three baseline sessions, participants complete 1,440 trials, yielding 240 trials per trial type — sufficient for reliable estimation of individual-level LBA parameters.

### PM Phase (Sessions 9–16)

The eight PM sessions are divided into two four-session blocks: one for the focal PM condition and one for the nonfocal PM condition. The order of conditions is counterbalanced across participants (half complete focal first, half complete nonfocal first).

**Transition between conditions.** At the start of the first session of each PM condition (sessions 9 and 13, in counterbalanced order), participants receive detailed instructions about the PM task. For the focal condition, they are told: "In addition to choosing the highest-reward color as before, watch for circles with an orange border. Whenever you see an orange border on any circle, press the spacebar as quickly as possible. You will earn 5 points for each detected PM cue. If you miss a PM cue and respond with a color key instead, you will lose 5 points." For the nonfocal condition, the instructions are analogous but refer to the star symbol. Instructions emphasize that the PM task is important and should not be neglected, but also that MCP performance matters — mirroring real-world situations where multiple intentions compete for limited attentional resources.

After instructions, participants complete a short practice block (48 trials, 25% PM frequency) to familiarize themselves with the dual-task demands. This higher-than-experimental PM frequency during practice ensures sufficient PM exposure before data collection begins. Data from practice blocks are excluded from analysis.

**Experimental PM sessions.** Each PM session consists of 480 trials organized into six 80-trial blocks with 30-second rest breaks. Within each session, PM cues appear on 15% of trials (72 PM trials per session). This frequency was chosen to balance two constraints: it is high enough to yield adequate PM trial counts for modeling (288 per PM condition across four sessions), yet low enough that PM cue occurrence remains somewhat unpredictable, preserving the ecological validity of the prospective memory demand.

**PM trial distribution.** The 72 PM trials per session are distributed as follows. Twelve PM trials are allocated to each of the six MCP trial types. Within each trial type, the PM cue is placed on one of the three circles at random with equal probability (1/3 each). For single-cue trial types (one rewarded, two zero-valued), this yields approximately 4 PM-on-rewarded and 8 PM-on-zero trials per type per session. For dual-cue trial types (two rewarded, one zero-valued), this yields approximately 4 PM-on-higher-value, 4 PM-on-lower-value, and 4 PM-on-zero trials per type per session. Across four sessions per PM condition, this produces approximately 288 PM trials total: roughly 96 PM-on-rewarded-circle trials (with variable reward values providing the key test of value-based competition) and 192 PM-on-zero-circle trials (providing a baseline for PM performance without direct MCP competition).

The remaining 408 no-PM-cue trials per session (68 per trial type) serve as the primary data for measuring monitoring costs on ongoing MCP performance.

**Feedback on PM trials.** When a PM cue is present and the participant presses the spacebar, the display reads "PM CUE CAUGHT! +5 points" in blue. When a PM cue is present but the participant presses a color key instead (PM miss), the display reads "+[X] points — PM CUE MISSED! −5 points" in orange, where X is the reward value of the selected color (or "Wrong color −2 points" if the MCP response was also incorrect, with the PM penalty appended). When no PM cue is present and the participant presses the spacebar (false alarm), the display reads "No PM cue! −2 points" in red. When no PM cue is present and the participant presses a color key, standard MCP feedback is given.

### Trial Timing

Each trial begins with a central fixation cross (500 ms), followed by the three-circle display, which remains on screen until a response is registered or 3000 ms elapses. Feedback is displayed for 800 ms. An inter-trial interval of 500 ms (jittered ± 100 ms) separates the feedback from the next trial's fixation cross. The total trial duration is approximately 2.3–2.8 seconds for typical response times, yielding a session duration of approximately 25–30 minutes for a 480-trial session.

### Optional Individual-Differences Battery (Session 16 or after final PM session)

In a separate 45-minute session following the final PM session, participants complete three computerized measures of cognitive ability. The **Operation Span task** (Unsworth et al., 2005) measures working memory capacity by requiring participants to verify math equations while remembering sequences of letters. The **Pattern Comparison task** (Salthouse & Meinz, 1995) measures processing speed by requiring speeded same–different judgments on pairs of line patterns. The **Stop-Signal task** (Verbruggen & Logan, 2008) measures response inhibition, yielding the stop-signal reaction time (SSRT) as the primary dependent variable. These measures are collected to test predicted dissociations between proactive control (hypothesized to correlate with working memory capacity) and reactive capacity sharing (hypothesized to correlate with processing speed). Participants are compensated an additional 100 DKK for completing this optional session.

## Trial Counts and Power Considerations

Across the entire experiment, each participant completes approximately 6,600 total trials: 720 training trials, 1,440 baseline trials, 1,920 focal PM trials (including 288 PM-cue-present trials), and 1,920 nonfocal PM trials (including 288 PM-cue-present trials). Training and practice trials are excluded from analysis.

For the critical PM conditions, the design yields the following trial counts per participant per PM condition (focal or nonfocal):

|Condition|Trials per session|Trials across 4 sessions|
|---|---|---|
|No-PM-cue trials (MCP monitoring cost)|408|1,632|
|PM cue on zero-value circle|~48|~192|
|PM cue on rewarded circle (value 1)|~8|~32|
|PM cue on rewarded circle (value 2)|~8|~32|
|PM cue on rewarded circle (value 3)|~8|~32|
|**Total PM-on-rewarded**|**~24**|**~96**|
|**Total PM trials**|**72**|**288**|

Given that previous applications of the PMDC model have achieved reliable parameter estimation with approximately 84 PM trials per condition per participant (Strickland et al., 2018), our design of 288 PM trials per condition substantially exceeds this benchmark. For the finer-grained PM-on-rewarded analysis, the relatively modest per-cell counts (~32 per value level per condition) are offset by the hierarchical Bayesian estimation framework, which pools information across participants to constrain individual-level estimates.

For MCP modeling, the 1,632 no-PM-cue trials per PM condition (272 per trial type) and 1,440 baseline trials (240 per trial type) provide ample data for stable estimation of accumulation rates and decision thresholds. These trial counts are comparable to those used in the standard four-color MCP paradigm (Oren et al., 2024), which collects approximately 5,760 trials across 12 experimental sessions with 10 trial types (576 per type).

## Dependent Measures

### Behavioral Measures

MCP performance is characterized by three measures computed on no-PM-cue trials only: accuracy (proportion of trials where the highest-value color is selected), response time for correct optimal selections, and suboptimal selection rate (proportion of dual-cue trials where the lower-value, but still rewarded, color is chosen). The primary measure of PM monitoring cost is the RT difference between no-PM-cue trials in PM blocks and matched trials in the baseline phase, computed separately for each trial type and PM condition.

PM performance is characterized by PM hit rate (proportion of PM-present trials where the spacebar is pressed), PM false alarm rate (proportion of no-PM-cue trials where the spacebar is pressed), and PM response time (RT on PM hit trials). PM hit rates are further analyzed as a function of PM cue location: PM-on-zero (baseline PM performance), PM-on-low-value, and PM-on-high-value, to test whether reward-driven MCP intention strength modulates PM detection.

### Computational Modeling

The core theoretical tests are implemented through a four-accumulator Linear Ballistic Accumulator (LBA; Brown & Heathcote, 2008) model with accumulators corresponding to the three color responses and the PM response. In baseline blocks, three accumulators race; in PM blocks, all four race on every trial (with the PM accumulator receiving near-zero input on no-PM-cue trials). The model is fit within a hierarchical Bayesian framework using Stan, with separate fits for the baseline, focal PM, and nonfocal PM conditions. Model comparison among threshold-only, drift-rate-only, and dual-mechanism variants will be conducted using the Widely Applicable Information Criterion (WAIC) and leave-one-out cross-validation (LOO-CV).

The key parameters of interest are: (a) how MCP accumulator thresholds shift from baseline to PM conditions (indexing proactive control), (b) how MCP accumulator drift rates change from baseline to PM conditions (indexing reactive capacity sharing), (c) whether these shifts differ between focal and nonfocal PM (testing the multiprocess prediction), and (d) how PM accumulator drift rates vary as a function of the reward value of the circle on which the PM cue appears (testing whether value-based MCP intention strength directly competes with PM retrieval).
